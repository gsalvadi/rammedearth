What Happens Next

I'll reach out to you personally within the next week. Before our conversation, I'd like you to consider:

Preparation for Our Conversation

  1. Your Project Reality: What are you actually building? Not the ideal version—the real one, with its constraints and possibilities.
  2. Your Timeline: When do you need to build, and why? Is this timeline coming from you or being imposed on you?
  3. Your Capacity: What can you actually commit to? Time, energy, financial resources, physical work. Be honest about all of it.
  4. Your Questions: Write down the specific technical questions you have. Not generic ones—the ones keeping you up at night.
  5. Your Fears: What scares you most about this project? The answer often reveals what matters most.

Understanding Consultation

This isn't about me telling you what to do. You already have instincts about earth—consultation is about helping you trust and refine them.

What we'll work on together:

Technical Guidance

  • Soil analysis and mix design for your specific location
  • Formwork design appropriate to your build scale
  • Construction sequencing and realistic timelines
  • Problem-solving when things don't go as planned (they won't)

Process Support

  • How to read what the material is telling you
  • When to push forward and when to pause
  • Building rhythm and sustainable work patterns
  • Integrating mistakes as learning, not failures

Practical Reality

  • Working within your actual constraints (time, money, help)
  • Managing permits and building codes
  • Finding or making necessary tools and materials
  • Knowing when you need additional skilled help
"The earth doesn't care about your plans. It will teach you what it wants to teach you, when it's ready. My role is to help you stay present for that teaching—and to keep you from making the mistakes that don't teach anything useful."

What To Do Right Now

While you wait for my email:

  • Document your site conditions—soil, climate, access, constraints
  • If possible, collect soil samples from your building location
  • Make some test bricks or small blocks with your soil
  • Start a project journal—you'll want this record later
  • Gather any questions, sketches, or ideas you have so far

A Word About Commitment

If we decide to work together, I'll ask you to commit to the process—not to a specific outcome. This means:

  • Showing up for the work, even when it's frustrating
  • Being honest about what you understand and what you don't
  • Trusting the material's feedback more than your assumptions
  • Accepting that this takes the time it takes

In return, I commit to giving you my full attention and the benefit of what I've learned through years of working with earth.
