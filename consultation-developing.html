Where You Are

You're in what I call the "awakening" phase. You've moved past thinking earth building is just an interesting idea, but you haven't yet developed the embodied knowledge that comes from sustained hands-on work.

This is actually a crucial stage. Many people rush past it, trying to jump straight to building projects. But the foundation you build now—through patient, focused practice—will determine everything that comes after.

"The material will teach you, but only if you give it time and attention. These practices aren't preparation for the real work—they ARE the real work."

Your Next Steps

Here are four practices that will move you from interested to truly capable. Do them in order, and don't rush.

Four Essential Practices

1. Build Something Small That Must Survive One Full Season

What: A garden wall, a bench, a small structure—something that will face real weather for an entire year.

Why: You'll learn what works and what fails. More importantly, you'll learn to read the signs before failure happens.

How: Start with something 3-4 feet tall maximum. Use local soil. Document it monthly with photos and notes. Watch what changes.

Duration: 12 full months from completion

2. Work With Earth for 30 Days Without Tutorials

What: Daily practice with earth—mixing, testing, forming—without following anyone else's instructions.

Why: Tutorials teach you techniques. Direct experience teaches you principles. You need the principles.

How: 30-60 minutes per day. Different soil ratios, different moisture levels, different compaction. Just observe and document what happens.

Duration: 30 consecutive days

3. Document What Goes Wrong, Not What Goes Right

What: Keep a failure journal. Every crack, collapse, weak spot, surprise—write it down with brutal honesty.

Why: Failure is information. Most people hide from it or make excuses. You need to mine it for what it reveals.

How: Photograph every failure. Write what you think caused it. A month later, write what you NOW think caused it. Notice the difference.

Duration: Ongoing practice

4. Find Local Earth Builders and Observe Their Process

What: Spend time watching experienced builders work. Don't just see the techniques—see their rhythm, their decision-making, their relationship with mistakes.

Why: Books and videos can't teach you the embodied knowledge that comes from watching a skilled person work.

How: Check the directory. Reach out respectfully. Offer to help in exchange for observation. Ask questions that start with "how do you know when..."

Duration: At least 2-3 full work days

What You'll Learn

If you do these practices seriously, here's what will shift:

  • You'll develop an intuitive sense for soil consistency and moisture content
  • You'll understand compression and structural stability at a felt level
  • You'll recognize the difference between your plan failing and the material teaching
  • You'll build realistic timelines based on actual experience, not optimism
  • You'll know which questions to ask because you've encountered real problems

When You're Ready for Consultation

After you've worked through these practices—and I mean really worked through them, not just skimmed—reach out again. At that point, you'll have:

  • Specific questions based on your actual experience
