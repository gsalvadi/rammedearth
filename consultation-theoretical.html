The Gap

Based on your responses, you're interested in rammed earth as an idea, a concept, perhaps even a philosophy. That interest is real—I'm not questioning it.

But earth building doesn't live in the realm of ideas. It lives in your hands, in the actual material, in the specific soil where you are. Until you close that gap, everything stays theoretical.

The Honest Truth

You can read every book about rammed earth. You can watch every video, study every technique, understand every principle intellectually. And you still won't know if this work is for you.

Because earth building isn't primarily an intellectual practice. It's a physical one. A tactile one. It asks for your hands, your back, your sustained attention to material reality.

You can't think your way into understanding earth. You have to touch it.

What You Need To Do

Not what you need to learn. Not what you need to read. What you need to DO.

Four Direct Practices - Start This Week

  1. Get Your Hands Dirty - Make Mud
    Not a test brick. Not a project. Just mud. Mix earth with water. Feel how it changes as you add moisture. Squeeze it. Form it. Let it dry on your hands. Notice everything about this experience. Do this for 30 minutes. Don't rush.
  2. Visit Existing Rammed Earth Buildings in Your Area
    Don't just look at photos online. Go stand next to a real rammed earth wall. Touch it. Put your hand flat against it. Feel its temperature, its texture, its mass. Stay there for at least 10 minutes. Notice what you notice.
  3. Start a Daily Practice of Working With Clay or Earth in Small Ways
    Every day for 30 days, spend 15 minutes with earth. Mix it, shape it, observe it drying. Don't try to make anything. Just develop a relationship with the material. Make this as consistent as brushing your teeth.
  4. Experience Failure With the Material
    Make something simple that will fail. A small cup. A tiny wall. Let it crack. Let it fall apart. Watch what happens. Document it. This failure is information. It's also initiation—until something you've made with earth has failed, you haven't really started.
"Earth building begins with earth itself, not ideas about earth. Close the gap between your curiosity and your hands. Everything else follows from there."

Why This Matters for Consultation

I can't effectively consult with someone who hasn't touched earth. Not because of elitism or gatekeeping, but because we won't have a shared language.

When I say "check the moisture content," you need to know what that feels like, not just what it means conceptually. When I say "compact in layers," you need to have felt the difference between properly compacted earth and loose earth.

Consultation builds on embodied knowledge. Right now, you don't have that foundation yet. Get it, then come back.

Resources for Getting Started

Hands-On Learning Opportunities

  • Natural building workshops in your region (search for local offerings)
  • Weekend courses at farms or eco-building centers
  • Volunteer on a rammed earth or natural building project
  • Contact builders in our directory and ask if you can observe

Finding Buildings to Visit

  • Search "rammed earth buildings near me" or "earthen architecture [your region]"
  • Contact local green building councils or sustainable architecture groups
  • Ask at architectural salvage yards or natural building supply stores
  • Check with local historical societies (older earth buildings may be documented)

Simple Materials to Start

  • Clay-rich soil from your property or nearby (free)
  • A bucket for mixing (you probably have this)
  • Water (obviously)
  • Your hands (the most important tool)

A Challenge

Here's what I want you to consider: Why haven't you done this yet?

I don't mean that as criticism. I mean it as genuine inquiry. You're interested enough to fill out a consultation form. Why not interested enough to spend 30 minutes making mud?

The answer to that question—whatever it is—will tell you something important about your relationship to this work.

Maybe you don't have time (which means this isn't actually a priority). Maybe you're afraid of doing it wrong (which means you need to experience that fear directly). Maybe you're waiting for the "right moment" (which means you're still treating this as theoretical).

Whatever your answer, sit with it. Then make a choice.
