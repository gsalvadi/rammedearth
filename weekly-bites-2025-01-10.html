Weekly Bites
January 10, 2025

This week, the same question surfaced in three different conversations across two continents: why does stabilized rammed earth keep winning architectural awards while unstabilized walls quietly perform better in humid climates?

A research team in Vietnam published soil testing data showing unstabilized walls in Hanoi handled moisture vapor better than their cement-stabilized counterparts. Meanwhile, an award-winning project in Arizona used 8% cement stabilization and appeared in Architectural Digest—beautiful, permanent, and completely unsuited for the climate it would have thrived in without the cement.

The pattern is familiar. What photographs well (crisp edges, uniform color, zero surface weathering) doesn't always perform well. What performs well (slight surface variation, natural patina, breathability) doesn't always photograph well. The gap between aesthetic legibility and material intelligence persists.

Perhaps the more interesting question isn't which approach is "better" but why we keep asking that question instead of: better for what, in which climate, for which purpose, evaluated over what timeframe?
