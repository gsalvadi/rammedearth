How to Choose the Right Contractor

When reaching out to contractors from the directory, here are important questions to ask:

Essential Questions

  • How many rammed earth projects have you completed?
  • Can I visit a finished project or speak with past clients?
  • What's your experience with projects similar to mine in scope and budget?
  • How do you handle local building codes and permits?
  • What's your typical timeline for a project of this size?
  • What warranties or guarantees do you offer?
  • How do you handle cost overruns or unexpected issues?
  • What's your payment structure and schedule?

Get multiple quotes: Talk to at least 3 contractors. Compare not just price, but communication style, experience level, and how comfortable you feel with them.

Check references: Actually call past clients. Ask about timeliness, communication, quality, and whether they'd hire the contractor again.

Trust your instincts: You'll be working with this person for months. If something feels off in initial conversations, pay attention to that.

Understanding Costs

Rammed earth construction typically costs more than conventional building methods. Be prepared for:

  • Higher material costs: Specialized formwork, equipment, and materials
  • Higher labor costs: Skilled rammed earth builders command premium rates
  • Longer timelines: Rammed earth takes more time than stick-frame or other methods
  • Site-specific factors: Soil conditions, access, climate all affect cost

Good contractors will be honest about these realities. If someone quotes significantly lower than others, ask why—it might indicate inexperience or hidden costs.

Alternative: Hybrid Approach

Some contractors offer a middle path where they handle the technical aspects while you provide some labor. This can reduce costs while ensuring quality. Ask contractors if they offer:

  • Owner-builder partnership models
  • Training + supervision packages
  • Phased work where they handle critical stages

This isn't right for everyone, but it might give you more hands-on involvement without the full responsibility of being an owner-builder.
