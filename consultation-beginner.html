Your Foundation: Four Essential Steps

These aren't preparation for earth building—they ARE earth building. Do them in order. Take your time.

1 Test Your Local Soil

Before anything else, you need to understand the soil where you are.

What to do:

  • Collect soil samples from different spots around your property or building site
  • Do a simple jar test: fill a clear jar 1/3 with soil, add water to top, shake vigorously, let settle for 24 hours
  • You'll see layers: gravel/sand on bottom, silt in middle, clay on top
  • Learn about clay content, organic matter, and what makes soil suitable for building
  • Contact your local soil conservation district—they often provide free testing and advice

Why this matters: Until you understand your local soil, everything else is abstract. This makes it real and specific to your place.

Time needed: 2-3 hours for collection and testing, plus 24-48 hours waiting time

2 Make 20 Test Bricks and Let Each One Fail

This is where most people want to rush. Don't. This step teaches you more than anything else you can do.

What to do:

  • Make simple brick-sized test blocks (6" x 3" x 3" roughly)
  • Use different soil mixes: more clay, more sand, different moisture levels
  • Try different compaction methods: hand-packed, tamped hard, barely compressed
  • Label each one (date, mix ratio, method)
  • Let them dry naturally - watch what cracks, what stays solid, what surprises you
  • After they're fully dry (1-2 weeks), test their strength - break them, drop them, soak them

Why this matters: Failure is information. Each crack teaches you something about water content, clay percentage, or compaction. This knowledge will save you from expensive mistakes later.

Time needed: 5-8 hours spread over 2-3 weeks

3 Read "Earth Construction Handbook" by Gernot Minke

Now—after you've gotten your hands dirty—read about the principles.

Why this book and why now:

  • It covers principles, not just techniques
  • You'll actually understand what you're reading because you've touched the material
  • It addresses different earth building methods so you can see where rammed earth fits
  • Reading it after hands-on work makes theory become practical knowledge

Why this matters: Books before practice give you information. Books after practice give you understanding.

Time needed: 6-10 hours of reading, plus however long you spend thinking about it

4 Spend Time With Earth Before Trying to Build With It

This sounds abstract, but it's the most important step.

What to do:

  • Mix earth with different amounts of water—just to feel how it changes
  • Hold dry clay in your hands, then slowly add water, sensing each stage
  • Form shapes without trying to "build" anything—just explore what the material can do
  • Notice how it responds to pressure, to drying, to moisture
  • Do this for 20-30 minutes several times, with no goal except observation

Why this matters: You're developing tactile intelligence. Your hands will learn to read moisture content, compression needs, and structural potential before your mind can articulate it. This embodied knowledge is what separates competent builders from people following instructions.

Time needed: 20-30 minutes, repeated 5-10 times over several weeks

"Earth building is not complicated. But it is deep. These steps might seem simple—and they are. That doesn't mean they're easy or quick. Give yourself permission to be a beginner. Give the material permission to teach you."

  • "The Hand-Sculpted House" by Ianto Evans - Philosophy and practice of earth building
  • "Building with Cob" by Adam Weismann & Katy Bryce - Different technique, same principles

Finding Help

  • Check our directory for builders in your area
  • Many builders offer introductory workshops—learning in person beats any book
  • Look for natural building courses at nearby farms or educational centers

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Starting with your dream project - Build small first. Really small.
  • Buying equipment before understanding basics - You don't need fancy tools yet.
  • Following tutorials without understanding why - Learn principles, not just steps.
  • Rushing through the learning phase - This time you spend now saves years of frustration.
  • Ignoring local building codes - Research this early, not when you're halfway built.

What's Next?

Work through these four steps at your own pace. Keep a simple journal of what you observe, what surprises you, what fails, and what questions arise.

When you've completed them—and I mean really completed them, not just checked them off—you'll be in a much stronger position to:

  • Know if earth building actually resonates with you
  • Ask specific, useful questions based on real experience
  • Understand whether you want to pursue consultation or a workshop
  • Make realistic plans for a building project

At that point, feel free to reach out again. You'll have a foundation to build on—literally and figuratively.
