While You Wait: Resources by Professional Stage

Regardless of where you are in integrating rammed earth into your practice, here are resources that might help. Take what's useful.

New to Rammed Earth (Architect/Engineer/Builder)

  • Get hands-on with small test builds before committing to client projects
  • Study existing rammed earth structures in person—touch the walls, understand the material
  • Read: "Building with Earth" by Gernot Minke and "Rammed Earth" by David Easton
  • Attend a natural building workshop to develop tactile understanding
  • Connect with experienced practitioners in the directory

Some Experience, Deepening Knowledge

  • Document your current process: formwork systems, labor hours, material costs
  • Identify specific technical bottlenecks—this is where knowledge transfer is most valuable
  • Experiment with variations: different soil mixes, formwork designs, compaction methods
  • Build a library of test results and failures—this becomes your knowledge base
  • Network with other professionals in the directory to share insights

Experienced Professionals Looking to Optimize

  • Analyze specific technical challenges: formwork efficiency, labor optimization, quality control
  • Consider soil testing protocols and how to standardize them for your practice
  • Develop training systems for your team—knowledge transfer works best with team commitment
  • Share your experience on Substack—the community benefits from professional insights
  • Focus consultation conversations on the specific technical gaps you've identified

The Hands-On Requirement

This bears repeating: effective technology transfer requires you (or your team) to get hands-on with the material. Not just supervise. Not just design from a distance. Actually touch the earth, feel the formwork, understand the process physically.

If that doesn't fit your working style, that's fine—but it means technology transfer isn't the right service for you. The directory has skilled contractors who handle full execution.

If you're committed to hands-on learning, then we have something to work with.
